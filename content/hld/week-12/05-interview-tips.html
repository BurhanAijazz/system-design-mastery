Interview Anti-Patterns and Tips

This is the meta-guide: how to use everything you have learned in an actual 45-minute system design interview. Covers the 10 deadly anti-patterns, time management, and how to structure your response for maximum impact.

The 10 Deadly Anti-Patterns

1. Jumping to Solution

Wrong: "Let's use Kafka and Redis and Cassandra and..." Right: "Before designing, let me clarify requirements and estimate scale." The interviewer wants to see you ask questions first.

2. No Estimation

Wrong: "Let's just use a database." Right: "With 50M DAU and 10 requests/user, we need ~6K QPS. A single PostgreSQL handles ~5K QPS, so we'll need sharding or read replicas." Numbers separate senior from junior candidates.

3. Over-Engineering

Wrong: Google-scale infrastructure for 1K users. Right: "At current scale, a single server handles this. At 100K users, we add caching and read replicas." A $10K/month architecture for a $1K/month problem is a bad design.

4. Under-Engineering

Wrong: "Single MySQL and cache everything" for 5M QPS. Right: "At 5M QPS, we need sharding. Here's my partitioning strategy." The interviewer gave you a scale for a reason.

5. No Trade-offs

Wrong: "We should use Cassandra." (end of discussion) Right: "Cassandra gives high write throughput but loses strong consistency. PostgreSQL with sharding gives ACID but more operational complexity. Given our write-heavy workload, I'd go with Cassandra."

6. Monologue Mode

Wrong: Talking for 20 minutes without pause. Right: "Does this high-level architecture make sense before I dive deeper?" The interview is a conversation.

7. Ignoring Hints

Wrong: Interviewer says "What about failure cases?" and you continue with the happy path. Right: "Great point -- let me address what happens when node X fails." Hints are free points.

8. Buzzword Bingo

Wrong: "Microservices, Kubernetes, service mesh, gRPC, event sourcing, CQRS, blockchain..." Right: Only mention technologies you can explain in depth. Buzzwords invite follow-up questions you might not be able to answer.

9. Drawing Before Thinking

Wrong: Immediately drawing boxes and arrows. Right: Verbally outline the approach first, then draw. You will end up erasing and redrawing otherwise.

10. Perfectionism

Wrong: Spending 15 minutes on the database schema. Right: "Here's a simplified schema. I can refine if needed." 45 minutes is not enough for a perfect design. Cover breadth first.

Positive Patterns to Follow

  • Structure your answer: Always follow DERSC (Define, Estimate, Architect, Scale, Confirm)
  • Think out loud: The interviewer cannot give you credit for thoughts you do not share
  • Check in frequently: "Should I go deeper on the database design or move to scaling?"
  • Use numbers: Every design decision should be justified with back-of-envelope math
  • Discuss trade-offs: For every choice, briefly mention what you are giving up
  • Prioritize: Solve the core problem first, then add extensions

Time Management Checklist

# 45-minute interview breakdown:
# Minutes  0-5:  DEFINE requirements (ask questions!)
# Minutes  5-10: ESTIMATE scale (back-of-envelope)
# Minutes 10-25: ARCHITECT core design (API, data model, components)
# Minutes 25-35: SCALE (bottlenecks, caching, sharding, failure)
# Minutes 35-40: CONFIRM (trade-offs, extensions, wrap up)
# Minutes 40-45: Interviewer questions and deep dives

Final Advice

  • There is no single right answer -- the interviewer is evaluating your thought process
  • Demonstrating awareness of trade-offs matters more than picking the "optimal" solution
  • Practice with a timer: if you cannot finish DERSC in 40 minutes, you are going too deep in one area
  • The best candidates make the interviewer feel like they are designing alongside a colleague, not evaluating a student
